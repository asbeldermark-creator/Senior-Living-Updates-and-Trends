+18%
Food costs in senior living have increased roughly 18% since 2022, with protein, produce, and dairy seeing the sharpest increases. Operators are navigating a difficult balance: maintaining resident satisfaction (dining is consistently the #1 or #2 satisfaction driver) while controlling one of the few remaining variable cost categories. Plain-English Context

  • Strong culture + pay + advancement wins the talent war
  • Capacity constrained by staffing, not beds — real revenue risk
  • Menu simplification and cross-training help dining teams cope with vacancies

Plain-English Context

The traditional IL/AL/MC buckets don't reflect how real people age. A person might be physically capable but have early dementia. Another might need significant physical help but be cognitively sharp. The rigid system creates awkward transitions, inappropriate care levels, and billing mismatches. Forward-thinking operators are experimenting with care models organized around what someone actually needs[4][8] and is interested in — with billing and staffing that adapt as the person changes over time.

What's Driving It

  • Residents' needs don't fit neatly into IL/AL/MC labels
  • Aging-in-place expectations — residents want to stay as needs evolve
  • Higher-acuity AL residents need services overlapping with skilled nursing
  • Boomer consumer preference for autonomy and personalization

Operational Implication

  • Care plans must be dynamic — static annual assessments are obsolete
  • Billing must accommodate fluid service bundles, not flat-fee care levels
  • Staff must be cross-trained across a broader acuity range
  • Compliance reporting must track individualized plans at scale

Plain-English Context

The "missing middle" — people with moderate savings and a Social Security check — earn too much to qualify for Medicaid but not enough to afford median AL rates. NORC research found that by 2033, 54% of middle-income seniors will be financially unable to afford assisted living.[12][13] A Place for Mom's February 2026 survey found affordability is now the #1 concern for families searching for senior living. This is simultaneously a crisis for individuals and a $415B market opportunity for operators who can figure out how to serve this segment profitably.

What's Driving It

  • Rates rising faster than middle-income fixed retirement income can support
  • Home care costs also rising ($34/hr median in 2025)
  • Families delaying moves until crisis — arriving higher-acuity and costing more
  • Only 18% of families understand senior living costs before starting their search

Operational Implication

  • Operators serving the middle market will have a sustained demand pipeline
  • Operational efficiency — not luxury amenities — is the differentiator here
  • Transparent pricing and financial counseling tools are marketing advantages
  • Business model design: mixed-payer, mixed-acuity, creative service bundling

Plain-English Context

Medicaid funds long-term care for lower-income Americans. The "One Big Beautiful Bill" cut over $1 trillion from Medicaid over 10 years — the biggest cut in the program's history.[9][10] Most AL runs on private pay and is somewhat insulated. However, many AL communities rely on state Medicaid waiver programs to serve lower-income residents — those programs are now at serious financial risk. SNFs face the most acute exposure. New work requirements (80 hrs/month, effective Dec 31, 2026) and biannual eligibility rechecks (effective Jan 1, 2027) add administrative complexity across the board.

What's Driving It

  • OBBB signed July 4, 2025 — sweeping federal Medicaid cuts now in effect
  • 80-hr/month work requirements for Medicaid ages 19–64, effective Dec 31, 2026
  • Eligibility rechecks every 6 months (from 12) starting Jan 1, 2027
  • HCBS expected to be cut first by states facing revenue shortfalls

Operational Implication

  • AL operators insulated but not immune — state waiver programs at risk
  • Payer diversification toward private-pay and Medicare Advantage is urgent
  • MA capitation rate increase (+5.06%) offers a partial and growing offset
  • SNF operators face direct reimbursement erosion — admission pipeline could shrink
